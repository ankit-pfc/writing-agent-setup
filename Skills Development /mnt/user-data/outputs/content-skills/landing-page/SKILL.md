# Landing Page Writing

Builds on content-writing-core and marketing-copywriting. All parent rules apply. This skill adds the landing page-specific layer — conversion rate architecture, above-the-fold mechanics, value proposition clarity, and the hierarchy of elements that determine whether a visitor converts or leaves.

**One rule that overrides all others:**  
A landing page has ONE goal. Every sentence, headline, image, and CTA serves that goal and nothing else. The moment a second goal appears on a landing page, conversion rate drops.

---

## 1. Landing Page Anatomy

### 1.1 Section Hierarchy

```
1. Hero Section (above the fold)
   ├── Headline — the value proposition
   ├── Subheadline — the supporting context
   ├── Hero CTA — the primary conversion action
   └── Optional: hero image, social proof indicator

2. Problem Acknowledgment
   └── Show you understand the pain before selling the solution

3. Solution Overview
   └── What it is and how it solves the problem — specifically

4. Features → Benefits
   └── What it does, why that matters

5. Social Proof
   └── Testimonials, logos, case studies, data

6. Objection Handling
   └── Address the main reason the ideal customer wouldn't convert

7. Offer / Pricing (if applicable)
   └── Clear, specific, no ambiguity

8. Final CTA
   └── Repeat the primary CTA with any urgency/scarcity if genuine

9. FAQ (optional but recommended)
   └── 4–6 questions that address real barriers to conversion
```

### 1.2 The Fold Rule

"Above the fold" = what is visible before the visitor scrolls. This section has one job: make them want to scroll.

The fold must contain:
- A headline that states the core value proposition in 10 words or fewer
- Enough context that the visitor knows what this is and who it's for
- A CTA that makes the conversion action obvious (even if they don't take it yet)

The fold must NOT contain:
- The full argument for why to buy
- Multiple CTAs
- Navigation that leads away from the page
- Generic stock photography that adds no information

## 2. Headline Mechanics

The headline is the most important sentence on the page. It determines whether the visitor reads anything else.

### 2.1 Headline Criteria

A landing page headline must:
1. **State the specific outcome** the product/service delivers
2. **Imply who it's for** (or explicitly state it)
3. **Be true** — it cannot overpromise

A headline may also:
- State the mechanism ("…without [common pain of alternatives]")
- Include a timeframe ("in 14 days," "in one afternoon")
- Include a specificity signal ("for B2B SaaS teams")

### 2.2 Headline Formulas

| Formula | Structure | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Direct outcome | [Do/Get/Achieve] [Outcome] | Write Landing Pages That Convert |
| Outcome without pain | [Outcome] Without [Common Obstacle] | Grow Your Email List Without Paid Ads |
| Targeted direct | [Outcome] for [Specific Audience] | Content Strategy for Early-Stage Founders |
| Timeframe | [Outcome] in [Timeframe] | Launch Your First AI Agent in 3 Days |
| Question (sparingly) | [Direct Question the Reader is Asking] | Why Isn't Your Landing Page Converting? |

### 2.3 Headline Bans

- "Welcome to [Company Name]" — tells the reader nothing
- "The Future of [Category]" — meaningless
- "[Company Name]: We Are [Adjective]" — company-centered, not reader-centered
- Any headline that requires the visitor to already understand what the product is
- Clever wordplay that sacrifices clarity

### 2.4 Subheadline

The subheadline's job: extend the headline by adding the most important context it couldn't hold.

Structure: What it is, who it's for, or how it works — in one sentence.
Length: 15–25 words maximum.

## 3. Value Proposition Clarity

The visitor must be able to answer these three questions within 8 seconds of landing on the page:

1. **What is this?**
2. **Is this for me?**
3. **What do I do next?**

If any answer is unclear — the page fails before the persuasion even begins.

### 3.1 The "So What?" Test

For every benefit statement, ask "So what?" until you reach the reader's actual desired outcome.

Example:
- "Our platform has AI-powered content suggestions." → So what?
- "You'll write content faster." → So what?
- "You'll hit your publishing schedule consistently." → So what?
- "You'll grow organic traffic without burning out your team." → This is the actual outcome.

Write the actual outcome, not the intermediate step.

## 4. CTA (Call to Action) Design

### 4.1 CTA Principles

- **One primary CTA per page.** It may repeat, but it's always the same action.
- **The CTA text must describe the action AND imply the benefit.** "Start Free Trial" beats "Submit." "Get My Content Calendar" beats "Download."
- **CTA context matters.** Buttons directly below social proof convert better. CTAs with micro-copy beneath them ("No credit card required. Cancel anytime.") reduce friction.

### 4.2 CTA Text Patterns

| CTA goal | Strong text | Weak text |
|---|---|---|
| Free trial | "Start Free — No Card Required" | "Sign Up" |
| Lead magnet | "Get the Free Template" | "Download" |
| Demo | "See It in Action" | "Request Demo" |
| Waitlist | "Join the Waitlist" | "Submit" |
| Purchase | "Get Instant Access" / "Start Today" | "Buy Now" |
| Newsletter | "Send Me the Weekly Brief" | "Subscribe" |

### 4.3 CTA Micro-copy

The line directly beneath a CTA button carries significant weight. Use it to remove the biggest friction point:
- "No credit card required"
- "Cancel anytime. No lock-in."
- "Instant access. No waiting."
- "Free for 14 days. Then $29/month."
- "Join 2,400 teams already using this."

## 5. Social Proof Architecture

Social proof on a landing page follows a hierarchy of persuasive weight:

**Tier 1 — Named case study with specific metric**
> "Reduced content production time by 60% in the first month — Sarah K., Head of Content, Notion"

**Tier 2 — Named testimonial addressing a specific objection**
> "I was skeptical it would work for a team our size. It does." — James R., Founder, 5-person team

**Tier 3 — Aggregate data**
> "Trusted by 4,200+ content teams across 50 countries"

**Tier 4 — Logo bar (recognizable brand names)**
> Logos of notable customers (only if recognizable to the target audience)

**Tier 5 — Star ratings from named platforms**
> "4.9/5 on G2 from 230 reviews" (link to the G2 page)

**Placement rules:**
- Tier 1 or 2 testimonial should appear above the fold or immediately below it
- Logo bar near the top for B2B if the logos are recognizable
- Testimonials adjacent to the CTA (reduces friction at the conversion point)
- Case studies mid-page for long-form sales pages

## 6. Objection Handling on Landing Pages

The visitor's unspoken objections must be addressed before they leave. Identify the top 3 objections for the specific offer, then address each:

**Common objections and where to address them:**

| Objection | Where to address |
|---|---|
| "Is this for me?" | Headline / subheadline — explicit audience statement |
| "Does it actually work?" | Social proof section, case studies |
| "Is it worth the price?" | Pricing section — frame value before stating cost |
| "Is it too complex / takes too long?" | Feature descriptions, onboarding story |
| "What if it doesn't work for me?" | Guarantee, free trial, refund policy |
| "I've tried similar things" | Differentiation section — what makes this different |

**Objection language:** Name the objection directly. "Worried about the learning curve?" is more powerful than dancing around it.

---

## 7. Pricing Section

When pricing appears on a landing page:

- **Show value before showing price.** The reader should know exactly what they're getting before they see the cost.
- **Anchor pricing.** If there are tiers, present the higher tier first (or prominently) — it anchors expectations.
- **Remove friction from the price.** "$29/month" → "$1/day" if the daily equivalent is psychologically lighter.
- **Name what's included specifically.** Vague inclusions ("unlimited access") are less convincing than specific ones ("unlimited seats, API access, dedicated support").
- **A guarantee near the price eliminates risk.** "30-day money-back, no questions asked" immediately adjacent to the price increases conversion.

## 8. Page Length Decision

Landing page length depends on the commitment the visitor is being asked to make:

| Ask | Recommended Length |
|---|---|
| Email opt-in (low friction) | Short — 300–500 words, minimal copy |
| Free trial sign-up | Medium — 500–900 words |
| Low-cost product (<$50) | Medium — 600–1,000 words |
| Mid-price SaaS / course ($50–$500) | Long — 1,000–1,800 words |
| High-ticket offer (>$500) | Long-form — 1,800–3,500 words |

**Rule:** Longer copy is not inherently better. Length should match the persuasion required to overcome the friction of the commitment.

## 9. Landing Page Quality Gates

In addition to core and marketing copy quality gates:

**Gate L1 — The 8-Second Test**
Show the page to someone unfamiliar with it. In 8 seconds, can they answer: What is this? Is it for me? What do I do next? If not — fix the above-the-fold.

**Gate L2 — The Single Goal Audit**
Count the distinct actions a visitor could take. If there are more than one (not counting repeated CTAs) — remove the extras.

**Gate L3 — The "So What?" Test**
For every benefit statement, run the "So what?" chain until you reach the reader's actual desired outcome. Ensure that's what's written.

**Gate L4 — The Objection Coverage Check**
List the top 3 objections a qualified lead would have. Are all three addressed somewhere on the page? If not — add them.

**Gate L5 — The Social Proof Specificity Test**
Find every testimonial and social proof element. Is each one specific enough to be credible? Generic praise ("Great product!") adds noise. If it can't be made specific — remove it.

**Gate L6 — The CTA Text Test**
Read each CTA button text. Does it describe the action AND imply the benefit? Could a more specific phrase replace it? If yes — improve it.
