# Marketing Copywriting

Builds on content-writing-core. All core rules apply. This skill adds the persuasion and conversion layer — the psychology, structures, and techniques that move people from awareness to action without manipulation.

**Core distinction:**  
Marketing copy is not lying dressed up as writing. It is the honest presentation of a genuine value in the most compelling, reader-centered way possible. The moment copy deceives, it destroys the trust it was trying to build.

---

## 1. The Persuasion Framework

Every piece of marketing copy addresses a reader who has:
1. **A problem** they are aware of (or can be made aware of)
2. **A desired state** they want to reach
3. **Obstacles** standing between their current state and the desired state
4. **Skepticism** — rational resistance to claims they've heard before

Effective copy addresses all four in sequence.

### 1.1 The Buyer Awareness Ladder

Content and copy should match where the reader is on this ladder:

| Stage | Reader's state | Copy's job |
|---|---|---|
| Unaware | Doesn't know they have a problem | Create awareness — name the pain before offering the solution |
| Problem-aware | Knows the problem, not the solution | Validate the problem, introduce the solution category |
| Solution-aware | Knows the solution type, not your brand | Position your specific solution as superior |
| Product-aware | Knows your brand, not convinced | Address objections, provide social proof, make the offer clear |
| Most aware | Ready to buy | Get out of the way — frictionless path to action |

**Rule:** Pitching a product to an unaware audience fails. Copy must first bring the reader to the right awareness stage before making an offer.

## 2. The Core Copywriting Structures

### 2.1 AIDA — Classic Framework (Use for Email, Ads, Awareness Content)
```
Attention — stop the reader; the hook that interrupts the scroll
Interest — connect to something they already care about
Desire — make them want what you're offering
Action — tell them exactly what to do next
```

### 2.2 PAS — Problem-Agitate-Solution (Use for Email, Longer Copy)
```
Problem — name the specific, felt pain
Agitate — make the cost of the problem vivid (without exaggerating)
Solution — introduce the thing that solves it specifically
```

**Warning on Agitate:** There is a line between helping someone understand a real cost and manufacturing anxiety for manipulation. Stay on the honest side of it. Exaggerated stakes destroy credibility with sophisticated readers.

### 2.3 PASTOR — Full Email or Campaign Framework
```
Problem — what hurts
Amplify — stakes if unsolved
Story — real example of someone in this situation
Transformation — what changed and why
Offer — what you're presenting
Response — the ask
```

### 2.4 FAB — Feature-Advantage-Benefit (Use for Product Copy)
```
Feature: What it is or does (the factual property)
Advantage: How this feature performs better than alternatives
Benefit: What this means for the reader's life/work
```

**Rule:** Readers don't buy features. They buy what features do for them. Always convert feature → benefit. Always name both.

## 3. Email Copy

### 3.1 Subject Lines

Subject lines determine open rate. They have one job: earn the open. Nothing else.

**Subject line principles:**
- 35–50 characters (displays fully on mobile)
- Specific beats clever (specific is more interesting, not less)
- Curiosity gap: imply there's something worth knowing without revealing everything
- No deceptive subject lines that misrepresent the email content
- First-name personalization: use when genuine, skip when it feels mechanical

**Subject line patterns:**

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| Direct benefit | "Your conversion rate problem has a simple fix" |
| Curiosity gap | "The thing about landing pages we stopped doing" |
| Numbered | "3 email patterns that lift open rates" |
| Question (genuine) | "Are you using GEO yet?" |
| News / update | "We changed how [feature] works — here's why" |
| Social proof | "What 200 of our customers did differently" |
| Re-engagement | "[Name], it's been a while" |

**Subject line bans:**
- ALL CAPS subject lines
- Excessive punctuation!!!!
- "RE:" or "FWD:" as fake reply framing
- Misleading previews designed to trick opens ("You've been selected...")
- "Don't open this email" reverse psychology (overused)

### 3.2 Email Body Structure

```
[Opening line — earns continued reading. Not "Hi, I'm writing to tell you..."]
[Bridge — connects subject line promise to body]
[Body — delivers on the subject line, addresses the reader's specific situation]
[Single CTA — one ask, specific, with a reason to act]
[Close — natural, not corporate]
[P.S. (optional) — the most-read line after the subject line. Use for the most important thing.]
```

**Email copy rules:**
- One email, one goal. If you have two goals — write two emails.
- The CTA should appear only once (or twice in very long emails — at middle and end)
- Write in second person ("you") throughout
- Paragraphs: 1–3 sentences maximum
- No headers in plain-text emails
- Preview text (the line visible before the email is opened) should extend the subject line, not repeat it

### 3.3 Email Sequence Logic

| Email # | Stage | Job |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Welcome | Deliver on the opt-in promise. Set expectations. One action. |
| 2–3 | Education | Useful content. No sell. Build trust. |
| 4 | Soft offer | Introduce the solution in context. Light CTA. |
| 5–6 | Deeper education | Proof, case study, demonstration. |
| 7 | Direct offer | Clear, honest, specific. |
| 8 | Objection handling | Address the one reason they haven't acted. |
| 9 | Final | Last chance framing — only if honest (real deadline or limit). |

## 4. Ad Copy

### 4.1 Principles Across Ad Platforms

- **One hook, one claim, one CTA.** Ads that try to do more than one thing do none of them.
- **Match the creative to the platform's native tone.** LinkedIn copy is different from Meta copy.
- **Specificity beats enthusiasm.** "Saves teams 4 hours per week" outperforms "Saves time!"
- **The image/creative carries 80% of the attention.** The copy captures it.

### 4.2 Google Search Ad Copy

- **Headline 1:** Primary keyword as close to verbatim as possible (matches search intent)
- **Headline 2:** Key benefit or differentiator
- **Headline 3:** CTA or offer
- **Description:** Expand the benefit, include social proof if available, include a secondary CTA
- Character limits: 30 per headline, 90 per description — precision required

### 4.3 Meta / Social Ad Copy

Structure:
```
[Hook — pattern interrupt OR pain statement]
[Proof or specificity — the credibility sentence]
[Offer — what they get]
[CTA — one action]
```

Length: Short (3–4 sentences for cold traffic), longer acceptable for warm audiences who recognize the brand.

### 4.4 LinkedIn Ad Copy

More formal than Meta. Decision-makers reading professional context.
- Open with a professional pain point, not a lifestyle hook
- Social proof carries more weight (name the company type or role, not just the outcome)
- CTA should feel earned: "See the case study" over "Buy now"

## 5. Objection Handling Copy

Every potential customer has objections. Copy that ignores them doesn't convert. The objections are always some version of:

1. **Price:** "It costs too much" → Address ROI, comparison to cost of the problem
2. **Time:** "I don't have time to implement this" → Address how minimal the time investment is
3. **Trust:** "I've tried things like this before and they didn't work" → Specificity, proof, guarantee
4. **Relevance:** "This isn't for me / my situation" → Specificity about the exact customer profile
5. **Urgency:** "I'll look at it later" → Only use genuine urgency/scarcity, never manufactured

**Rule:** Address the single biggest objection the target reader has. Don't try to address all five in one piece of copy.

## 6. Social Proof Patterns

Social proof is not optional — it is one of the highest-value elements in marketing copy.

**Types, ranked by effectiveness:**

| Type | Effectiveness | Use When |
|---|---|---|
| Named case study (person + company + specific result) | Highest | You have one and permission to use it |
| Specific testimonial with result ("[X] outcome in [Y time]") | High | Better than generic praise |
| Named testimonial ("John Smith, CMO at Acme") | High | Authority of the person adds value |
| Data ("10,000 teams use this") | Medium | When the number is meaningful |
| Anonymous testimonial | Low | Only when nothing better is available |
| Generic praise ("Loved it!") | Very low | Avoid — adds noise, not signal |

**Rule:** Specific social proof ("Reduced churn by 23% in 90 days — Priya M., Head of Growth at Clearbit") outperforms 10 generic testimonials.

## 7. Tone Calibration for Marketing Copy

### 7.1 The Empathy Balance

Marketing copy fails in two directions:
- **Too enthusiastic:** Sounds like a pitch, triggers skepticism
- **Too neutral:** Fails to generate desire or emotional resonance

The correct register: **empathetically direct.** The tone of a trusted advisor who has seen the problem before and knows the solution works.

### 7.2 Words That Earn Trust vs. Lose It

**Trust-earning:**
- Specific numbers, named sources, defined timeframes
- Honest limitations ("This works best for X, not for Y")
- Proof before claim ("Here's what happened, here's why we think it did")

**Trust-losing:**
- Superlatives without evidence: "best," "most powerful," "revolutionary"
- Urgency without truth: "Only 2 left!" (when there are thousands)
- Vague benefits: "Transforms your business"
- Testimonial phrases: "This changed my life" (generic, unverifiable)

## 8. Marketing Copy Quality Gates

In addition to core quality gates:

**Gate M1 — The Awareness Stage Match**
Does this copy match where the target reader actually is on the awareness ladder? Copy pitched to the wrong stage doesn't convert.

**Gate M2 — The Single Goal Test**
What is the one action this piece of copy is asking for? If there are two — cut to one.

**Gate M3 — The Feature→Benefit Conversion**
Find every feature claim. Does it have a corresponding benefit stated explicitly? If not — add it.

**Gate M4 — The Objection Test**
What is the main reason the target reader wouldn't take the CTA? Is that objection addressed somewhere in the copy? If not — address it.

**Gate M5 — The Honesty Check**
Find every superlative, urgency claim, and social proof element. Is each one verifiable and true? If not — make it specific and true, or remove it.
