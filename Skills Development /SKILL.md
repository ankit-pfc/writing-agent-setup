# Content Writing Core

Every piece of content in this system — regardless of format, platform, or purpose — is built on these foundational principles. Subskills add rules on top. They never override the core.

---

## 1. The Foundational Hierarchy

When writing any piece of content, execute in this order:

1. **Clarity first.** If the reader has to re-read a sentence to understand it, rewrite the sentence.
2. **Accuracy before style.** Factual precision outweighs elegant phrasing. If they conflict, be precise.
3. **Audience fit.** The right level of complexity, vocabulary, and assumed knowledge for the specific reader.
4. **Purpose alignment.** Every sentence earns its place by serving the piece's single stated goal.
5. **Structure as communication.** How the content is organized is itself an argument.

---

## 2. Universal Clarity Rules

### 2.1 Sentence-Level

- **Active voice by default.** Passive voice is permitted only when the subject is genuinely unknown or the passive is more precise.
- **One idea per sentence.** If a sentence contains "and" or "but" connecting two full thoughts, consider splitting.
- **Shortest accurate word.** "Use" over "utilize." "Show" over "demonstrate." "Start" over "commence."
- **Specific over general.** "A 14-week study of 200 participants" over "research shows." Numbers earn credibility.
- **No filler openers.** Sentences beginning with "It is important to note that," "In today's world," "As we all know" — delete them and start with the real information.
- **Subject-verb proximity.** The verb should follow the subject as closely as possible. Long embedded clauses between subject and verb kill readability.

### 2.2 Paragraph-Level

- **Lead with the claim, follow with the support.** First sentence states the point. Remaining sentences prove it.
- **Maximum 5 sentences per paragraph for web.** Digital reading is non-linear. Shorter paragraphs reduce drop-off.
- **One paragraph, one job.** It introduces, or it develops, or it transitions. Not all three.
- **Paragraph breaks are decisions.** Each break tells the reader: "this thought is complete, a new one begins." Don't break arbitrarily.

### 2.3 Document-Level

- **Every section earns its place.** Cut any section that doesn't advance the reader toward the stated goal.
- **The ending must do something.** Not summarize. Synthesize, imply, open a new question, or call to action — depending on content type.
- **Front-load value.** The most important information appears first. This is true at the document level, section level, and sentence level.

## 3. Audience Calibration

Before writing, define:

| Parameter | Question | Impact on writing |
|---|---|---|
| Knowledge level | What does this reader already know? | Vocabulary, assumed context, explanation depth |
| Intent | Why are they reading this? | Information architecture, emphasis |
| Trust level | Do they trust the source? | How much evidence is needed, how direct to be |
| Action desired | What should they do after reading? | Whether and how to include a CTA |
| Attention budget | How much time will they give this? | Length, skimmability, hook strength |

**Rule:** Write for one reader, not an average of readers. If the audience spans expertise levels, choose the more expert end and define terms on first use.

## 4. Research Synthesis Standards

### 4.1 Sourcing Hierarchy
1. Original research, first-party data, primary sources
2. Peer-reviewed studies, institutional research
3. Reputable journalism and long-form reporting
4. Expert commentary from named, credentialed sources
5. Industry reports from established organizations
6. Aggregators, secondary sources — use only when primary is unavailable

### 4.2 Using Evidence in Content

- **Cite specifics, not generalities.** "A 2025 McKinsey study found X" over "research suggests X."
- **Attribute, don't just assert.** Unsourced claims carry the credibility weight of the author alone.
- **Evidence follows the claim.** State your point, then support it. Not the other way around.
- **One strong piece of evidence over three weak ones.** Stacking weak citations doesn't equal one strong one.
- **Recency matters.** Prioritize data from the last 2 years for fast-moving fields. Older data is valid for foundational concepts.

### 4.3 What NOT to Do
- Never fabricate statistics or present plausible-sounding data without a verifiable source
- Never attribute a quote to a source without confirmation it was said
- Never use vague authority phrases: "experts say," "studies show," "research indicates" — without naming the study

## 5. Structure Templates — Universal

### 5.1 The Direct Structure (Recommended Default)
```
[Core claim / answer / value — what this piece delivers]
[Context — why this matters]
[Development 1 — first layer of evidence or explanation]
[Development 2 — second layer]
[Complication or nuance — where it gets more complex]
[Synthesis — what all of this means]
[Ending — action, implication, or open question]
```

### 5.2 The Problem-Solution Structure
```
[Problem — stated specifically, as the reader experiences it]
[Stakes — what happens if unsolved]
[Solution overview — what exists to address it]
[Solution in depth — how it works]
[Evidence — why it works]
[Action — what to do next]
```

### 5.3 The Comparison Structure
```
[What is being compared and why it matters]
[Option A — honest assessment]
[Option B — honest assessment]
[The dimensions of comparison]
[Synthesis — when each is appropriate]
[Recommendation — clear, conditional]
```

## 6. Voice and Tone Calibration

### 6.1 Universal Voice Standards (All Content)
- **Confident but not arrogant.** State what you know without hedging what you've verified.
- **Direct, not cold.** Short sentences don't mean unfriendly ones.
- **Specific, not technical.** Technical accuracy doesn't require jargon. Define terms on first use.
- **Authoritative, not preachy.** Present the evidence and let it do the work.

### 6.2 Tone Modulation by Context
| Content Type | Tone Direction |
|---|---|
| Web content / blog | Conversational + expert. The knowledgeable colleague. |
| SEO / GEO | Clear + structured. The reliable reference. |
| Marketing copy | Empathetic + direct. The honest salesperson. |
| Landing page | Confident + benefit-focused. The trusted guide. |
| News writing | Neutral + precise. The accurate reporter. |

## 7. Universal Quality Gates

Every piece passes these before it is considered complete:

**Gate 1 — The Clarity Test**
Read each sentence. If you had to re-read it, rewrite it.

**Gate 2 — The Redundancy Test**
Read each sentence and ask: "Does this add something the previous sentence didn't?" If no — cut it.

**Gate 3 — The Specificity Test**
Find every instance of "many," "some," "often," "usually," "most," "experts," "research." Each must be replaced with a specific number, name, or citation — or deleted.

**Gate 4 — The Ending Test**
Does the ending summarize what just came before? If yes — rewrite. The ending should leave the reader in a different place than before the last paragraph, not the same place restated.

**Gate 5 — The Purpose Test**
What is the single job this piece of content is supposed to do? Does every section serve that job? Cut what doesn't.

## 8. Universal Banned Phrases

These phrases appear in weak content across all categories. Remove on sight:

**Opener bans:**
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "As we all know..."
- "It goes without saying..."
- "It is important to note that..."
- "Throughout history..."
- "Since the dawn of time..."
- "In recent years..."
- "More and more people are..."

**Body bans:**
- "At the end of the day" (unless literal)
- "Game-changer" / "paradigm shift" / "disruptive" (unless critically defined)
- "Leverage" (non-physical use)
- "Synergy"
- "Cutting-edge" / "state-of-the-art" / "best-in-class"
- "It's no secret that..."
- "The fact of the matter is..."
- "With that being said..."
- "Moving forward"
- "Circle back"
- "Deep dive" (as verb)
- "Unpack"
- "Holistic approach"
- "Robust" (outside of engineering)
- "Empower" (in generic use)
- "Journey" (when applied to business processes)

**Closer bans:**
- "In conclusion, we can see that..."
- "I hope this article helped you..."
- "If you found this useful, please share..."
- "Stay tuned for more..."

## 9. Content Length Standards

| Content Type | Recommended Range | Over-length signal |
|---|---|---|
| Blog / Web post | 800–2,000 words | Repetition, padding, tangents |
| SEO / GEO article | 1,200–2,500 words | Keyword stuffing, thin development |
| Marketing email | 150–400 words | More than one CTA, excessive context |
| Landing page | 300–1,500 words (depends on complexity) | Arguments that belong in a blog post |
| News article | 300–800 words | Background that exceeds the news event |
| LinkedIn post | 150–1,300 characters | Everything |

**Rule:** The right length is the minimum needed to achieve the content's purpose. Never pad to hit a word count. Never cut to hit a brevity target if the logic requires the length.
