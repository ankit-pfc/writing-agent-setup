# Marketing Copywriting — Subskill

**Inherits from:** content-writing-core  
**Adds:** Awareness levels, desire architecture, email mechanics, ad copy formulas, conversion psychology, the Schwartz framework, Ogilvy headline science

---

## 1. The Foundation: Desire Architecture (Eugene Schwartz)

The most important insight in all of copywriting, from *Breakthrough Advertising* (1966):

> "The copywriter does not create desire. He channels and directs it toward his product."

Mass desire exists before your product. Your copy is the channel. The question is never "how do I make them want this?" — it's "what do they already want, and how does this product get them there?"

**Implication for every piece of marketing copy:**
Before writing a word, answer: what does this reader already want that this product/offer serves? Start with that want — not the product.

---

## 2. The Five Awareness Levels (Schwartz)

Every reader exists at one of five awareness levels. Copy written at the wrong level fails regardless of quality.

| Level | What the Reader Knows | Lead With |
|---|---|---|
| **Unaware** | Doesn't know they have the problem | A story or provocative observation — not a product pitch |
| **Problem Aware** | Knows the problem, not the solution | The problem, made urgent and specific |
| **Solution Aware** | Knows solutions exist, not yours | Your mechanism — what makes your approach different |
| **Product Aware** | Knows your product, hasn't bought | Proof, social validation, offer details |
| **Most Aware** | Ready to buy, needs the trigger | The deal, the deadline, the final push |

**How to identify the awareness level:**
- Cold audience (paid ads, new email list, organic social): assume Unaware or Problem Aware
- Warm audience (existing subscribers, retargeting): Solution Aware or Product Aware
- Hot audience (cart abandoners, high-intent clicks): Product Aware or Most Aware

**The most common copy mistake:** Writing Product Aware copy to an Unaware audience. The reader has no context for why they should care.

## 3. The AIDA Framework (Modernized)

The classic framework, updated for digital:

**A — Attention:** The hook that earns the next line. (Ogilvy: "On average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy.")

**I — Interest:** Develop the idea; make the reader feel seen. The problem or desire, made specific and personal.

**D — Desire:** Show the gap between where they are and where they could be, and position your product as the path. Show, don't tell: let the reader feel the desired state, not just hear about it.

**A — Action:** One specific ask. Not "learn more, sign up, or buy" — choose one. The more options, the lower the conversion.

## 4. Email Copy Mechanics

### 4.1 Subject Lines (The Single Most Important Variable)

Subject line performance determines whether the rest of the email exists. The goal: open, not click. The subject line sells the open; the email sells the click.

**What works:**
- **Specificity:** "How I got 847 email subscribers in 30 days" beats "How to grow your email list"
- **Intrigue without clickbait:** Hint at the value; don't reveal it all. But make it believable.
- **Personal/direct:** Subject lines that look like they're from a person, not a company, outperform branded subject lines in most B2C contexts
- **Short (under 50 characters):** Mobile preview shows ~40-50 characters. Front-load the important words.
- **Testing:** Test one variable per send. Subject line A vs. subject line B. No other variables.

**What doesn't work:**
- ALL CAPS
- Excessive punctuation!!!
- Fake RE: or FWD: prefixes (destroys trust)
- Vague promises ("You won't believe this")
- "Newsletter" in the subject line

### 4.2 Email Opening (First 2 Sentences)

The email opens in the preview pane. The first 2 sentences determine whether the reader clicks through to read.

Pattern: **Specific + relevant.** Either: a specific fact that startles, a specific question the reader is asking themselves, or a specific observation from recent experience that is instantly recognizable.

NOT: "I've been thinking about [vague topic] lately." This is the email equivalent of "In today's fast-paced world."

### 4.3 Email Body Structure by Goal

| Goal | Structure | Length |
|---|---|---|
| Nurture/educate | Story → Insight → Soft CTA | 200-400 words |
| Product announcement | Context → Product reveal → Benefit → CTA | 150-300 words |
| Promotional | Problem → Agitate → Solution → Proof → CTA | 300-500 words |
| Re-engagement | Direct ask ("Are you still here?") → Value reminder → Reduce/exit option | Under 200 words |

### 4.4 CTA Rules

- One CTA per email. If you must have two, make one primary and one text-only.
- The CTA button should describe the outcome, not the action: "Get the guide" not "Click here." "Start my trial" not "Sign up."
- Repeat the CTA in text form near the bottom for readers who scroll past buttons.

## 5. Ad Copy (Google, Meta, LinkedIn)

### 5.1 Google Search Ads

The reader is already searching for a solution. Awareness level: Solution Aware or Product Aware.

**Headline formula:** [Primary keyword] + [Key differentiator] + [Call to action]
- Max 30 characters per headline
- Responsive search ads: write 10–15 headlines, Google tests combinations
- First headline should match the search query as closely as possible (quality score)

**Description formula:** [Specific benefit] + [Proof point] + [CTA]
- Max 90 characters per description
- Expand on what the headline promised; don't repeat it
- Include the primary keyword at least once

**The specificity principle (Ogilvy):** "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise..." wins over "The quietest car." Specific beats superlative in ad copy. "Saves 3 hours per week" beats "Save time."

### 5.2 Meta / Social Ads

Cold traffic. Unaware to Problem Aware audience. The scroll stop is the entire job of the first line.

**Primary text (first line = the scroll-stopper):**
- The Uncomfortable Truth Hook (Dan Koe): Name what they know but haven't said
- The Counterintuitive Claim: Violate their assumption in one sentence
- The Specificity Hook: A number, a person, a specific situation they recognize

**Visual-copy alignment:**
- The visual and the copy should NOT be redundant. Either: visual is the hook, copy develops it. Or: copy is the hook, visual creates the emotion.
- One job per element.

**CTA alignment:**
- Match the awareness level. Unaware audience → "Learn more" not "Buy now."
- Product Aware → "Shop now," "Start free trial"

### 5.3 LinkedIn Ads

B2B context. The reader is a professional. Copy that would work on Meta often feels too casual; copy that works on LinkedIn often feels too stiff on Meta.

**What works on LinkedIn:**
- Peer-to-peer register: "I spent 3 years running content strategy for a Series B startup. Here's what I learned."
- Credibility through specificity, not title-dropping
- The "for [specific role]" targeting echo: "For founders who manage their own content..."

## 6. Social Media Copy (Brand Voice)

### 6.1 The Single-Idea Rule

Every social post = one idea. Not "here are three things about X." One thing about X, fully expressed.

### 6.2 Platform-Specific Rules

**Instagram caption:**
- Front-load before the fold: the first line visible without tapping "more" is the hook
- Emojis: use as visual punctuation, not decoration. One emphatic use beats five scattered ones.
- Hashtags: 5-10 relevant, placed at the end or in a comment

**LinkedIn post:**
- First line visible before "see more" is the entire ad for the post
- Line breaks are aggressive — one idea per line in the first 3 lines
- No hashtag walls; 3-5 targeted hashtags max
- The comment section is part of the content strategy — ask a genuine question that invites professional perspective

**X / Twitter:**
- 280 characters is a constraint that forces precision
- The hook is the tweet; the reply thread is the expansion
- Engagement comes from: specificity, counterintuition, or being the first to say something people were thinking

## 7. Product Description Copy

The job of product copy is not to list features. It is to make the reader feel the product in use.

**The features-to-benefits bridge:**
- Feature: "Noise-canceling headphones"
- Benefit: "You disappear into your work. The open office disappears."

**The sensory translation:**
Take the product's physical/functional attributes and translate them into the reader's experience of having it. Not "made from 400-thread-count Egyptian cotton." Translate: "The first morning you sleep on these, you'll understand why people talk about them."

**The specific social proof integration:**
Testimonials in product copy work better when they are specific and slightly unexpected. "I didn't expect to cry on a Tuesday afternoon. This book." > "Life-changing book. Highly recommend."

## 8. Copy Editing Checklist

Before any copy ships:

**Schwartz check:**
- [ ] Is this copy written at the reader's actual awareness level?
- [ ] Does the opening meet the reader where they are, or does it assume knowledge they don't have?

**Ogilvy check:**
- [ ] Is the headline specific enough to be believed?
- [ ] Could this headline be used by a competitor? (If yes, it's not specific enough.)

**Clarity check (Orwell):**
- [ ] Is there a shorter word anywhere? Replace it.
- [ ] Is there a passive construction? Activate it.
- [ ] Does any sentence contain more than one idea? Split it.

**CTA check:**
- [ ] One CTA only?
- [ ] Does the CTA describe the outcome, not the action?
- [ ] Is the CTA consistent with the awareness level?

**Commitment check:**
- [ ] Does this copy say something specific that could be wrong? (If not, it says nothing.)
- [ ] Is there any hedge that exists to protect the writer rather than serve the reader? Remove it.
