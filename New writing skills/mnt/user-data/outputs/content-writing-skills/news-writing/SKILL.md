# News Writing — Subskill

**Inherits from:** content-writing-core  
**Adds:** Inverted pyramid structure, the 5 W's + H, press release mechanics, newsworthiness criteria, source attribution, journalistic objectivity, AP Style essentials

---

## 1. The Foundational Principle: The Inverted Pyramid

News writing inverts the conventional essay structure. The most important information comes first. Supporting detail follows in decreasing order of importance. The least essential context is last.

**Why this structure:**
- Historically: editors cut from the bottom. The story is never killed by a cut.
- Practically: readers bail from the bottom. They always get the essential information.
- Digitally: Google's featured snippets and AI citations pull from the top. The answer must be there.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│          LEAD (Most Important Information)          │  ← The answer
│              Who, What, Where, When, Why            │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│              BODY (Supporting Detail)               │  ← The context
│        Quotes, Evidence, Background, Context        │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│           TAIL (Least Critical Information)         │  ← The extra
│         History, Boilerplate, Related Links         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## 2. The Lead (Lede)

The lead is the first paragraph. It contains the entire story, compressed. A reader who reads only the lead knows the essential fact.

**The 5 W's + H checklist for the lead:**
- **Who** — the person, organization, or entity the story is about
- **What** — what happened or what is being announced
- **Where** — location (when relevant)
- **When** — timing (date, time, or timeframe)
- **Why** — the significance or reason
- **How** — the mechanism (when relevant and brief)

Not every lead needs all six. Identify which are essential to this specific story and lead with those.

**Lead length:** 25-40 words maximum. One sentence or two short sentences. The temptation to include more is always wrong.

**Lead types:**

| Type | When to Use | Example Structure |
|---|---|---|
| **Summary Lead** | Breaking news, press releases | [Who] [did/announced what] [when], [significance in one clause] |
| **Anecdotal Lead** | Feature news, human interest | Opens with a specific scene or person; broader significance in paragraph 2 |
| **Statistic Lead** | Data-driven stories | Startling number + what it means + source attribution |
| **Quote Lead** | When the quote IS the news | Direct quote + attribution + why this quote matters |
| **Question Lead** | Use sparingly; only genuine questions | The question the story answers; paragraph 2 begins the answer |

**The summary lead formula (most common):**
```
[Subject] [verb] [object], [context], [significance].
```
Example: "Anthropic announced Claude 4, its most capable AI model to date, citing significant improvements in reasoning and code generation."

## 3. Body Structure

### 3.1 The Nut Graf

In feature news and press releases, the "nut graf" (nutshell paragraph) follows the lead. It answers: "Why does this story matter? Why now? Why should the reader care?"

Position: Paragraph 2-3, after the hook but before the supporting detail.

**The nut graf formula:**
- The broader significance of the specific event in the lead
- The context that makes this event notable
- The stakes for the reader or the field

### 3.2 Supporting Paragraphs

Each subsequent paragraph adds one piece of supporting information, in decreasing importance:
1. Additional detail that expands on the lead's key claim
2. Direct quotes from the most important source
3. Background context
4. Secondary quotes or supporting data
5. Historical context
6. Boilerplate / related information

### 3.3 Quote Rules

Quotes in news writing serve one purpose: to convey the voice, opinion, or reaction of a source in a way that paraphrase cannot. They are not used to state facts — those are paraphrased with attribution.

**Good quotes:** Opinion, reaction, unique framing, authority statement
- "This is the most significant advance in the field in a decade," said [Name], [Title].

**Replace with paraphrase:** Facts, explanations of process, anything that reads as a complete factual statement
- Weak: "The company said it had 3.2 million users."
- Better: The company has 3.2 million users, according to its announcement.

**Attribution position:**
- Preferred: [Quote], [Name] said. (Attribution at end)
- Acceptable: [Name] said [quote]. (Attribution at start for context)
- Avoid: "Said [Name]" (inverted attribution sounds archaic)

### 3.4 Attribution and Source Credibility

**Hierarchy of source credibility:**
1. Primary documents (official statements, filings, verified releases)
2. On-the-record named sources (most credible)
3. Official spokesperson quotes
4. On-background sources (referenced without name but with title/role)
5. Anonymous sources (use only when essential; explain why anonymity was granted)

**Attribution phrases:**
- "according to" — for documents, reports, data
- "said" — neutral, standard attribution for quotes
- "announced," "confirmed," "stated" — for official announcements
- "claimed," "alleged" — for unverified assertions or contested claims
- Never: "admitted" (implies guilt), "revealed" (implies secret), "exclaimed" (editorial)

## 4. Press Release Structure

Press releases are news writing for distribution. Journalists receive hundreds; the format signals professionalism and makes the journalist's job easier.

```
FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE (or: EMBARGOED UNTIL [Date/Time])

HEADLINE (News value, not marketing language — same rules as news headlines)
SUBHEADLINE (Additional context that the headline couldn't hold)

DATELINE — [CITY, Date] — Lead paragraph (full inverted pyramid lead)

BODY:
  → Nut graf: why this matters
  → Quote from primary company spokesperson (CEO, Founder, or most senior relevant person)
  → Supporting detail: product features, data, timeline
  → Quote from secondary source (customer, partner, investor) if available
  → Background / context

ABOUT [COMPANY]: Boilerplate — 2-3 sentences, standard across all releases

CONTACT INFORMATION:
  Name, Title, Email, Phone
```

**Press release writing rules:**
- The headline is not an ad. It is a news headline. Test: would a journalist use this as a headline?
- No superlatives in the headline: "world's first," "revolutionary," "game-changing" — unless provably true
- The company quote should say something the company would want quoted, not a generic placeholder
- Boilerplate is standard and not edited per release — maintain consistency
- Length: One page maximum for a standard announcement. Two pages maximum for major news.

## 5. Newsworthiness Criteria

Before writing, assess whether the story meets the standard that makes it worth publishing. A story is newsworthy when it scores high on at least 2-3 of these:

| Criterion | Questions to Ask |
|---|---|
| **Timeliness** | Is this happening now? Is there a reason to publish today specifically? |
| **Significance** | How many people are affected? How deeply? |
| **Proximity** | Is this close to the reader's geography, industry, or community? |
| **Conflict** | Is there a genuine dispute, challenge, or opposing force? |
| **Human interest** | Is there a person's story that makes the abstract concrete? |
| **Novelty** | Is this genuinely new — not just framed as new? |
| **Prominence** | Does this involve a well-known person, company, or institution? |

## 6. AP Style Essentials

News writing follows AP Style (Associated Press). The most commonly needed rules:

**Numbers:**
- Spell out one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above
- Spell out any number that begins a sentence: "Forty-seven people attended."
- Percentages: use % symbol with numerals (47%), "percent" with spelled-out numbers

**Dates and times:**
- Months with six or more letters are abbreviated: Jan., Feb., Aug., Sept., Oct., Nov., Dec.
- March, April, May, June, July are never abbreviated
- Use numerals: Jan. 15, 2026 (not January 15th, 2026)
- Times: 3 p.m., 11 a.m. (lowercase, periods)

**Titles:**
- Formal titles before names are capitalized: President Biden, CEO Jane Smith
- Titles after names or without names are lowercase: Jane Smith, chief executive officer

**Punctuation:**
- Serial comma: NOT used in AP Style ("red, white and blue" not "red, white, and blue")
- Quotation marks: periods and commas inside. Colons and semicolons outside.

**Company names:**
- Use the company's official stylization on first reference: iPhone, eBay, YouTube
- Do not add apostrophes to brand names for possessives that the brand itself doesn't use

## 7. News Writing Quality Checklist

**Structure:**
- [ ] Lead contains the essential 5 W's (all that apply) in under 40 words
- [ ] Nut graf appears in paragraph 2-3 and explains why this matters now
- [ ] Information is ordered from most to least important throughout
- [ ] Every paragraph advances the story (no padding, no recaps)

**Accuracy and attribution:**
- [ ] Every claim either comes from a named source or is common knowledge
- [ ] Direct quotes are verified and attributed with name + title
- [ ] Numbers and data include source attribution
- [ ] No editorial language in news sections (admitted, exclaimed, revealed without basis)

**Clarity (Orwell/AP):**
- [ ] Lead is one or two sentences, under 40 words
- [ ] No jargon without immediate plain-language explanation
- [ ] Active voice throughout (passive only when the receiver of action is more important than the actor)
- [ ] AP Style applied to dates, titles, numbers

**Headline:**
- [ ] Headline is a news statement, not a marketing promise
- [ ] Specific enough to stand alone (reader knows the story from the headline alone)
- [ ] Present tense for current events; past tense for completed events
