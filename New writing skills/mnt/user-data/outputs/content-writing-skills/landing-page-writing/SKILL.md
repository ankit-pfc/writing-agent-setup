# Landing Page Writing — Subskill

**Inherits from:** content-writing-core, marketing-copywriting (awareness levels, desire architecture, Schwartz, Ogilvy)  
**Adds:** Above-the-fold architecture, objection handling, proof sequencing, conversion-specific CTA mechanics, the single-conversion discipline

---

## 1. The Single Commandment

A landing page has one job. One.

Not "tell them about us." Not "rank for keywords." Not "build brand awareness." One conversion action: sign up, buy, register, join the waitlist.

Every word on the page is either moving the reader toward that action or it is working against it. There is no neutral copy on a landing page.

---

## 2. The Above-the-Fold Equation

The fold is the bottom of the visible screen before the reader scrolls. What lives above it must answer four questions in under 5 seconds:

1. **What is this?** — The offer, in plain language
2. **Who is it for?** — The reader must recognize themselves
3. **What is the outcome?** — Not a feature list; the specific result
4. **What do I do?** — The CTA must be visible without scrolling

**The above-the-fold formula:**

```
[HEADLINE: The outcome + for whom + in what timeframe/conditions]
[SUBHEADLINE: The mechanism — how this delivers the outcome + the key differentiator]
[CTA BUTTON: Outcome language, first-person phrasing]
[Trust signal: 1 line — e.g., "Used by 3,400 founders" or "No credit card required"]
[Optional: hero image or video that shows the product in use, not stock photography]
```

**Headline archetypes that work for landing pages:**

| Archetype | Formula | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Outcome + Mechanism | [Achieve X] through [method] | Grow your newsletter to 10,000 subscribers through daily writing |
| Problem Solved | End [specific pain] without [common sacrifice] | End cold outreach anxiety without sounding like a robot |
| For Whom | [Outcome] for [specific person] | The content system built for founders who hate content |
| Bold Claim + Proof | [Specific outcome]: Proven by [evidence] | 40% more email opens: Data from 12,000 campaigns |

**What kills above-the-fold:**
- Clever headlines that don't say what the product does
- Headline + subheadline that say the same thing
- CTA that says "Submit," "Click here," or "Learn more"
- A hero image that is abstract or metaphorical
- More than one CTA above the fold

## 3. Page Architecture — The Full Scroll

The reader who scrolls past the fold is objection-handling. They want the product but have reasons not to act. The rest of the page is a sequenced response to those objections.

**Standard conversion page architecture:**

```
SECTION 1: ABOVE THE FOLD
└── [Headline / Subheadline / CTA / Trust signal]

SECTION 2: THE PROBLEM (Problem Aware readers enter here)
└── Make the problem vivid, specific, and felt — not announced
└── "You know the feeling when..." — situational empathy
└── Do not name competitors. Name the problem they cause.

SECTION 3: THE SOLUTION (Your product enters here)
└── Introduce the product as the mechanism that solves Section 2's problem
└── Do NOT list features. Show the transformation.
└── Before/After is the fundamental structure of this section

SECTION 4: THE MECHANISM (Why it works)
└── The specific thing that makes your approach different
└── Schwartz: Name and explain the mechanism. Belief in the mechanism > belief in the product.
└── Simple diagrams, screenshots, or video walkthroughs belong here

SECTION 5: SOCIAL PROOF (Trust building)
└── The proof that validates the mechanism's claims
└── Sequence: Testimonials → Case studies → Data → Logos
└── Specificity rules: "I made $12,400 in the first 60 days" > "This changed my business"

SECTION 6: FEATURES → BENEFITS
└── Only after the reader believes the mechanism
└── Format: Feature name → What it does → Why it matters (the benefit)
└── Never more than 6-8 features; each must serve the core promise

SECTION 7: OBJECTION HANDLING
└── Name and address the 3-5 most common objections directly
└── FAQ format works well here
└── The objections to address: price, time, trust, relevance, urgency

SECTION 8: CTA (Repeated)
└── Same offer as above the fold — no surprises
└── One more social proof element (number of users, or testimonial)
└── Reduce friction: remove one barrier here (cancel anytime / free trial / money-back)

SECTION 9: FINAL CTA (The close)
└── The last reader who reaches here is the most skeptical — or the most ready
└── Gary Halbert's Undeniable Stack: summarize every reason to act, then ask
└── Make the cost of inaction visible: "What happens if nothing changes?"
```

## 4. Conversion Copywriting — Sentence-Level Rules

### 4.1 The "You" Ratio

Good landing page copy refers to "you" (the reader) far more often than "we" or "our product." A rough target: 3:1 reader to brand references.

"You'll never worry about open rates again" > "Our system improves open rates."

### 4.2 Specificity = Believability

The Ogilvy principle applies hardest here. Vague claims are discounted; specific claims are believed because the reader assumes no one would be specific about something they couldn't defend.

- "Saves time" → "Saves 3 hours per week" → "Saves 3 hours per week (our users report spending 12 minutes daily vs. the industry average of 45 minutes)"
- Each level is more persuasive than the last.

### 4.3 The Present-Tense Transformation

Describe the desired future state in present tense, as if it's already true for the reader:

"You wake up to 47 new subscribers. You check the analytics on your phone before getting out of bed. The campaign ran while you slept."

This is not a promise — it's a visualization technique. The reader's brain begins to own the outcome.

### 4.4 Social Proof Hierarchy (Most to Least Persuasive)

1. **Video testimonial from a specific, named person with specific results** — most credible because hardest to fake
2. **Written testimonial with full name, company, photo, and specific outcome**
3. **Case study with before/after data and timeline**
4. **Aggregate data:** "3,400 customers," "4.9/5 from 800 reviews"
5. **Logo wall from recognizable companies**
6. **Awards and press mentions**

*The mistake:* Leading with logos (least persuasive) before testimonials (most persuasive). Start with the human proof.

### 4.5 Objection Handling — The 5 Universal Objections

Every conversion decision faces these five objections. The page must address all five:

1. **"I don't believe this will work."** → Mechanism section + case studies with data
2. **"This won't work for me specifically."** → Testimonials from people exactly like them + FAQ addressing their specific context
3. **"I can't afford it."** → ROI framing + payment options + money-back guarantee
4. **"I don't have time."** → Time investment clarity + how quickly they see results
5. **"I'll do this later."** → Cost of delay made visible + time-limited element if genuine

## 5. CTA Copy Science

The CTA button is the single most tested element in conversion optimization. The principles:

**First-person language:** "Start my free trial" consistently outperforms "Start your free trial." The reader is completing their own sentence.

**Outcome language:** The button should describe what the reader gets, not what they do.
- "Get instant access" > "Download"
- "Build my content system" > "Sign up"
- "Start writing better" > "Register"

**Friction reducers (the line under the button):**
"No credit card required." / "Cancel anytime." / "Free for 14 days."
These are not afterthoughts — they remove the last hesitation at the moment of decision.

**Button design rules (beyond copy):**
- Color that contrasts with the page (the eye needs to find it instantly)
- Large enough to be the obvious next action
- Visible without scrolling at least 3 times on the page

## 6. Landing Page Editing Checklist

**Structure:**
- [ ] Single conversion goal throughout
- [ ] All four above-the-fold questions answered within 5 seconds
- [ ] CTA repeated minimum 3 times on page
- [ ] All 5 universal objections addressed somewhere on the page

**Copy quality:**
- [ ] "You" appears at least 3x more than "we"/"our"
- [ ] Every claim is specific (no "saves time" — only "saves X hours")
- [ ] Testimonials are named, specific, and contain results
- [ ] Mechanism is named and explained (not just "our proven system")

**Awareness level alignment:**
- [ ] Awareness level of the traffic source identified
- [ ] Page opens at the correct level (not pitching to an unaware audience)

**Schwartz check:**
- [ ] Does the opening channel existing desire or try to create new desire?
- [ ] Does the mechanism section explain WHY the product works, not just WHAT it does?

**Ogilvy check:**
- [ ] Does the headline make a specific, believable promise?
- [ ] Is the headline the best of at least 10 options written before choosing?

**Halbert close:**
- [ ] Does the final section stack every reason to act?
- [ ] Is the cost of inaction visible?
- [ ] Is the friction at the point of conversion minimal?
